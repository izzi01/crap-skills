# Anti-Generic Visual Review

This skill is for visual critique when a design feels like "AI crap": sleek enough to pass, but too anonymous to remember.

Its job is to identify visual sameness, explain why the design feels generic, and propose concrete changes that create identity, clarity, and believable craft.

## When to use

Use this when the user wants to:
- critique a landing page, homepage, hero, dashboard, or marketing mockup
- make a design feel less AI-generated or less template-driven
- remove Dribbble-shot polish that hurts clarity or credibility
- create a more ownable visual language
- sharpen art direction so the page looks like *this brand*, not any brand

## What generic AI visual crap looks like

Treat the design as suspicious if several of these appear together.

### 1. Polished sameness
- purple/blue gradients with no brand reason
- soft glow blobs everywhere
- floating cards on foggy backgrounds
- generic 3D-ish illustrations or abstract mesh forms
- stock-looking people cropped into premium-looking layouts

### 2. Empty visual hierarchy
- everything is big, bold, and loud
- hero, nav, cards, badges, and CTA all compete equally
- contrast is used for style, not for reading order
- the page scans like a moodboard, not a decision path

### 3. Decoration without information
- glassmorphism, shadows, blur, rings, and sparkles that add no meaning
- cards that repeat the same layout rhythm with no importance differences
- icon rows, logo rows, and stat chips that could belong to any startup

### 4. Fake depth / fake premium
- too many gradients, shadows, and radii trying to signal "high-end"
- surfaces feel inflated rather than structured
- polished effects are doing the work that typography and composition should do

### 5. No ownable brand cues
- nothing in the page would help someone identify the brand if the logo were removed
- color, type, imagery, and layout all feel borrowed from current trend kits
- there is no memorable tension, stance, or personality

### 6. AI-generated image language
- over-perfect people, uncanny lighting, impossible reflections
- too-smooth hero visuals with no editorial point of view
- imagery chosen for vibe only, not for trust, context, or domain truth

## Review method

Inspect in this order.

### 1. First-glance identity
Ask:
- If the logo disappeared, what still feels specific to this brand?
- What is memorable after a 2-second glance?
- Is the first impression clarity, trust, and intent — or just trend fluency?

If the answer is mostly style adjectives, the page is probably generic.

### 2. Hierarchy and decision flow
Ask:
- What should the eye hit first, second, third?
- Does the page guide action, or just present a beautiful surface?
- Are emphasis and spacing helping decisions?

Prefer designed reading order over ambient polish.

### 3. Information-bearing visuals
Ask:
- Which visual choices actually communicate something?
- Which effects could be removed with no loss of meaning?
- Are cards, icons, and imagery carrying domain truth or just filling space?

### 4. Brand ownability
Ask:
- Which 2-3 elements are ownable enough to become signatures?
- Is the typography generic-safe or chosen with intent?
- Are colors serving recognition, not just attractiveness?

### 5. Image truthfulness
Ask:
- Do images feel domain-true, or just premium-looking?
- Would a real customer trust this visual world?
- Are the images supporting proof, context, or emotion in a believable way?

## Rewrite / redesign rules

### Replace trend effects with structure
- Fewer glows, more hierarchy
- Fewer floating layers, more deliberate alignment
- Fewer decorative cards, more meaningful grouping

### Replace anonymous polish with ownable choices
- choose one distinctive compositional move
- choose one image treatment with a reason
- choose one typographic attitude that matches the brand
- make the palette feel chosen, not default

### Replace vibe-only imagery with evidence-bearing imagery
- show real context, real people, real artifacts, real environments
- if using art direction, tie it to the domain and audience
- if an image is only saying "modern", it is probably expendable

### Replace premium theater with believable trust
- proof blocks should feel operational, not ornamental
- trust cues should be readable and anchored, not floating as decoration
- visual restraint often feels more premium than layered effects

## Anti-generic visual checklist

Before calling the design strong enough, confirm:
- there is a clear reading order
- the hero has one dominant idea
- the CTA wins at the right moment
- visuals support information, not just atmosphere
- at least 2-3 brand-ownable signals exist beyond the logo
- imagery feels believable and domain-specific
- trend effects are used sparingly and intentionally
- the page would still have character in grayscale and without glow effects

## Common red flags to call out directly

Name these plainly when present:
- "gradient sludge"
- "template hero"
- "anonymous SaaS card grid"
- "fake premium glass"
- "AI-uncanny imagery"
- "Dribbblified hierarchy"
- "moodboard instead of decision flow"

## Output format

Use this structure when critiquing.

## Anti-generic visual review: [Name]

### Why it feels generic
- **Sameness signals:** ...
- **Hierarchy failures:** ...
- **Decorative noise:** ...
- **Weak brand-ownable cues:** ...
- **Image truthfulness risks:** ...

### What already works
- ...

### Highest-leverage changes
1. **[Change]** — what to remove, what to emphasize, and why
2. **[Change]** — what to simplify, sharpen, or replace
3. **[Change]** — how to make the visual language more ownable

### Concrete redesign moves
- Remove ...
- Keep ...
- Increase ...
- Reduce ...
- Replace ... with ...

### Visual direction test
Describe the page after revision in one sentence. If that sentence could still describe ten other startups, the direction is not specific enough.

## If reviewing multiple variants

Compare them on:
- silhouette and first impression
- dominant hierarchy move
- image strategy
- CTA posture
- brand memory

If two variants could swap hero sections without anyone noticing, they are too generic.
