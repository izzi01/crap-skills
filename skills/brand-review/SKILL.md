# Brand Review

This skill merges anti-generic copy critique with anti-generic visual critique.

Use it when a brand experience looks polished enough to ship but still feels anonymous. The goal is to find where the work could belong to almost anyone, then replace that sameness with audience truth, proof, hierarchy, and ownable character.

## What this skill protects against

It is designed to catch two failure modes that often appear together:

1. **Generic copy crap**
   - vague claims
   - faceless audience language
   - presentation-speak or internal concept language
   - aspiration without proof
   - variants collapsing into the same safe voice

2. **Generic visual crap**
   - gradient sludge
   - fake-premium glass and glow
   - dribbblified hierarchy
   - decorative card grids with no information value
   - AI-uncanny imagery
   - polished layouts with no ownable brand cues

## When to use

Use this skill when the user wants to:
- review a homepage, landing page, or hero section
- critique a mockup, screenshot, or Figma direction
- make a design feel less generic, less AI-generated, or less template-driven
- strengthen both messaging and visual identity at once
- compare variants and preserve meaningful differences
- turn subjective brand critique into concrete edits and, when useful, repo-native tests

## Core review model

Always review both **message** and **surface**.

A page is strong when:
- it names a real audience
- it names a real problem
- it shows how the offer works
- it gives believable proof
- it guides the eye clearly
- it uses visuals that carry meaning, not just polish
- it has 2-3 ownable cues that still remain if the logo disappears

If one side is strong and the other side is generic, the page still feels generic.

## Review sequence

Inspect in this order.

### 1. Audience + first-glance identity
Ask both:
- Who is this for, exactly?
- Does the copy name them explicitly?
- If the logo disappeared, what would still feel specific to this brand?
- After a 2-second glance, what is memorable?

If the copy talks to nobody specific and the visuals look like trend defaults, call that out directly.

### 2. Problem + hierarchy
Ask:
- What real tension, confusion, fear, or desire is the page speaking to?
- What should the eye hit first, second, third?
- Does the hierarchy help the user decide, or just help the page look polished?

Prefer decision flow over moodboard energy.

### 3. Mechanism + information-bearing visuals
Ask:
- How does this product or service actually help?
- What process, steps, or operating model are visible?
- Which visual choices communicate information?
- Which effects could be removed with no loss of meaning?

If the answer is mostly style and vibe, the page needs structural work.

### 4. Proof + trust surfaces
Look for concrete proof such as:
- team, teacher, operator, or founder specificity
- hotline, Zalo, chat, consultation channel
- credentials, process steps, milestones, levels, turnaround windows
- real artifacts, real environments, real screenshots, real program outputs
- visual trust blocks that feel anchored, not ornamental

If proof is thin, either add evidence or soften the claim.

### 5. Ownability + variant differentiation
Ask:
- What 2-3 lines belong only to this brand?
- What 2-3 visual moves belong only to this brand?
- Why does each variant exist?
- Could these variants swap heroes, CTAs, or layouts without anyone noticing?

If yes, the work is too generic.

## Red flags to name plainly

### Copy red flags
- faceless audience language
- agency/presentation/meta wording
- mechanism-free promises
- filler CTA language
- interchangeable variant voice

### Visual red flags
- gradient sludge
- template hero
- anonymous SaaS card grid
- fake premium glass
- AI-uncanny imagery
- dribbblified hierarchy
- moodboard instead of decision flow

## Preferred callout vocabulary

When one of these patterns is present, prefer naming it with the blunt label itself rather than only describing it indirectly. Semantic equivalents are fine, but these labels are preferred because they are memorable and easy to compare across reviews.

### Preferred copy callouts
- `audience gaps`
- `copy genericness`
- `proof gaps`
- `mechanism-free promises`
- `agency/presentation/meta wording`
- `route-specific voice`
- `CTA posture`

### Preferred visual callouts
- `gradient sludge`
- `template hero`
- `anonymous SaaS card grid`
- `fake premium glass`
- `decorative noise`
- `weak brand-ownable cues`
- `visual silhouette`
- `anchored proof`
- `moodboard instead of decision flow`
- `dribbblified hierarchy`

If the page deserves a hard critique, do not soften the callout into polite abstractions. Name the failure mode directly.

## Rewrite / redesign rules

### For copy
- replace vague claims with audience-specific truth
- replace brand puffery with operational detail
- replace internal concept language with client language
- replace empty CTA text with a concrete next step

**Example**
- Bad: "Giải pháp toàn diện cho mục tiêu của bạn"
- Better: "Kiểm tra trình độ, xếp lớp phù hợp, lên lộ trình học và tư vấn bước tiếp theo cho hồ sơ du học."

### For visuals
- replace trend effects with hierarchy
- replace anonymous polish with ownable choices
- replace vibe-only imagery with evidence-bearing imagery
- replace premium theater with believable trust

**Example moves**
- fewer glows, more deliberate alignment
- fewer decorative cards, more meaningful grouping
- one distinctive compositional move instead of five trendy effects
- imagery that shows real context instead of generic aspiration

## What strong work looks like

A stronger brand page usually has these traits:
- the hero expresses one dominant idea
- the CTA wins at the right moment
- the copy sounds like it knows the reader's situation
- the proof feels operational and believable
- the visuals support reading order and trust
- the page still has personality in grayscale and without glow effects
- the brand would remain somewhat recognizable even if the logo were removed

## If the repo has tests

When reviewing code-backed pages, prefer encoding repeated critique as tests.

### Good test categories
1. audience specificity
2. domain/proof specificity
3. banned generic filler phrases
4. route differentiation
5. required trust/proof signals

### Testing rule
Do not weaken a detector because it found a real issue. Fix the page if the detector is telling the truth.

## Output format

Use this exact structure.

## Brand review: [Name]

### Why it feels generic
- **Audience gaps:** ...
- **Copy genericness:** ...
- **Hierarchy failures:** ...
- **Decorative noise:** ...
- **Proof gaps:** ...
- **Weak brand-ownable cues:** ...
- **Variant differentiation risks:** ...

### What already works
- ...

### Highest-leverage changes
1. **[Change]** — what to remove, sharpen, or add, and why
2. **[Change]** — what to simplify or restructure
3. **[Change]** — what would make the brand more ownable

### Concrete copy edits
- Replace ... with ...
- Name ... explicitly
- Add proof for ...

### Concrete visual edits
- Remove ...
- Reduce ...
- Increase ...
- Replace ... with ...
- Reorder ... so the eye lands on ... first

### Brand direction test
Describe the revised page in one sentence.
If that sentence could still describe ten other brands, the direction is not specific enough.

### Verification
- focused checks or tests run
- exact result
- exact failing or passing evidence
